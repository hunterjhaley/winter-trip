Japan: The Trip of Trips

Eight of us, one big week, three worlds and a Frozen castle: neon Tokyo, a hot-spring night staring at Mt. Fuji, and thousand-year-old Kyoto at peak fall color, stitched together by 200 mph bullet trains. Late November is the single best week of the year to be in Japan, and it's our week.

Nov 20 to 29 • zero school missed Tokyo • Mt. Fuji • Kyoto • Nara 8 travelers, ages 4 to 38 ~$29K of a $35K ceiling
Why this week, why Japan

We're aiming at Japan's best week

Late November is when Kyoto's fall foliage peaks (entire temple gardens in red and gold), the air turns crisp and clear, and Mt. Fuji hits its most visible season of the year. It's also our Thanksgiving break. That's not a coincidence we planned; it's one we get to cash in.

The shape of it

  • One Friday red-eye, three worldsOut Friday night after school, a quick LA connection, and we land Tokyo Sunday morning with the day ahead of us instead of behind us. That gained morning is exactly what lets us add Disney on a weekday with zero school missed. The route: 3 Tokyo nights up front with a full Disney day on Tuesday, 1 night at a Mt. Fuji hot-spring ryokan, 2 nights in Kyoto plus a Nara day, and 1 last Tokyo night before flying home Sunday. No backtracking, no wasted days.
  • The bullet trains are an attraction, not a transferTwo shinkansen rides at up to roughly 200 mph: Fuji country to Kyoto on Thanksgiving Day, and Kyoto back to Tokyo at sunset on the last full day. Window seats booked on the Fuji side both times.
  • No rental cars, on purposeJapan's trains are the best on earth and family-friendly: elevators everywhere, seats reserved, nobody parallel parks. We use prepaid transit cards for the city, reserved seats for the long hauls, and one clever trick below for the luggage.
  • The luggage trick that makes it all workJapan's luggage-forwarding service (takkyubin) ships our big bags from the Tokyo hotel straight to the Kyoto hotel for about $15 a bag. We travel to Mt. Fuji with daypacks only, the bags beat us to Kyoto, and nobody hauls eight suitcases up a mountain.

The daily rhythm (same system as always)

🌅 Early crew

Japan rewards the jet-lagged: temples and shrines are empty and magical at 7 to 8am. Whoever's up goes; it's a 15 minute walk back to the beds either way.

☕ Slow crew

Apartment-hotel breakfasts, no alarms, a wander window from 10 to noon. Convenience-store breakfast in Japan is genuinely part of the experience.

🤝 Together from noon

Mornings split by energy, afternoons run together: the big anchors (gardens, shows, deer, feasts, light museums) sit after lunch so early birds and sleepers both make every one. One meeting point per city, one time: noon at the lobby.

❤️ Built for the littles: everything on this route is stroller-flat and train-connected: escalators and elevators at every station, reserved seats on every long haul, a gondola instead of any climb at Fuji, and a konbini snack never more than a block away. The 4 and 6 year old set the pace, and the plan never feels slow for the teens doing it.
Jet lag, played honestly: Japan is 15 hours ahead. The play is simple: sleep on the plane, push through to 9pm local the first night, and the kids will wake up at 6am ready to beat every crowd in Tokyo, which is exactly what we want.

Before We Go

Hunter is booking everything, so this tab is the family half: what to pack, what to expect with money over there, and the handful of things each household handles before we fly. Read it once now and once the week before.

💰 Money over there: cash, cards, and the no-tipping country
  • The card truthCities take cards nearly everywhere now. Cash is still king at street stalls (the tanghulu run), small temple entries, some ramen shops, and the Nara mochi window. Plan on cards for big spends, cash for fun.
  • How much cashSkip exchanging dollars in the US entirely. Land, then pull yen from the 7-Eleven ATM in the arrivals hall (7-Bank ATMs take US debit cards, have English menus, and beat every exchange counter's rate). Pull about 50,000 yen per family to start, roughly $350, and refill at any 7-Eleven all week. Whole-trip cash for the group lands around $1,200 to $1,500.
  • Before we flyCall the banks with travel dates so cards don't freeze, and know each debit card's foreign ATM fee; a no-foreign-fee debit card in one wallet saves real money across a trip this size.
  • Transit moneyAdd a Suica card to each adult iPhone's Apple Wallet (2 minutes, reload with Apple Pay, works on every train, bus, vending machine, and konbini). Grab physical Welcome Suica cards at the airport for the kids.
  • Tipping: zeroJapan does not tip, anywhere, for anything; it can genuinely confuse or embarrass staff. The price is the price. Hardest habit of the trip for Texans.
🧰 Packing & prep (the two weeks before)
  • Visit Japan Web, all 8 of us, two weeks outJapan's official online pre-registration for immigration and customs. Hunter fills it once per traveler, everyone lands with a QR code, and the arrivals line becomes a scan instead of a form with jet-lagged littles. vjw.digital.go.jp
  • The meds auditOctober task, full rules in While We're There: original bottles, a month or less, and the banned list checked. Daily-prescription folks flag Hunter by September.
  • Pack like it's a crisp Texas fall, not a ski tripLayers for 40s to 60s, one real jacket each, hats and gloves for the Fuji night and dawn temple runs, and walking shoes broken in BEFORE the flight. This trip walks 12,000+ steps a day.
  • One big bag per person, one daypack eachForwarding is priced per bag, and the Fuji night runs on daypacks only. Every daypack carries a trash bag (no public cans) and a water bottle.
  • Wheels for the 4 year oldA compact travel stroller that folds onto trains, or a carrier, is the difference between the Kyoto day finishing and it ending at 2pm. Disney rents strollers on site too.
  • Plugs: good newsJapan uses the same two-prong outlets as the US. Phone chargers work as-is; only three-prong laptop plugs need a cheap adapter.
  • The shared photo albumOne iCloud or Google Photos shared album, all eight phones feeding it, created before wheels up. One link, every angle of the trip in one place.
  • The thank-you built inThis trip is a gift, so the gift goes back: the kids record one short thank-you clip at every stop (the toboggan-level moments: sumo, castle, deer), and in December the album becomes a printed photo book delivered to the grandmother who sent us. That's the plan, in writing, so it actually happens.
  • Souvenir envelopesEach kid gets a set cash envelope for the trip; when it's gone it's gone. Ends every gift-shop negotiation before it starts, and Japan's gift shops are world-class ambushes.
  • Two booking notes so nobody learns the hard wayAsk the ryokan for the children's kaiseki option for the 4 and 6 year old (a twelve-course adult dinner versus a 4 year old is a known battle), and check Fantasy Springs ride height minimums for the littles the week Disney tickets are bought.
  • The home frontBoth houses empty nine days over a holiday week: put names next to Maggie, the chickens, the mail, and Thanksgiving-week coverage at work before the calendar fills. Not glamorous, still the difference between relaxing over there and refreshing cameras.

💊 The medicine law that catches Americans

⚠️ Do the meds audit in October, not at the airport: Japan bans some everyday US medications. Sudafed and anything with pseudoephedrine is illegal to bring in, period. ADHD stimulants like Adderall are prohibited without advance government permission (a Yakkan Shoumei certificate, applied for weeks ahead). Some codeine products are restricted.
  • The safe patternEverything in original labeled bottles, no more than a month's supply, prescriptions photographed, and anything questionable checked against Japan's health ministry guidance in October. Tylenol, Advil, Benadryl, and kids' fever meds are all fine.
  • If anyone in the crew takes a daily prescriptionFlag it to Hunter by September so there's time to file for permission if it needs one. This is the single least-known trip-ruiner in Japan travel.
🍁 How not to stick out: the etiquette quicksheet
  • Shoes off where the floor changesRyokan, temples, some restaurants: if there's a step up and slippers waiting, shoes come off. Wear socks you like.
  • Trains are library-quietPhones on silent, conversations low, no calls. The littles get the whisper game.
  • Nobody eats while walkingBuy the tanghulu, stand by the stall, finish it, then walk. It's the custom everywhere.
  • There are no public trash cansGenuinely almost none. Every daypack carries a plastic bag; trash rides home to the apartment.
  • Escalators: stand left in Tokyo(Kyoto and Osaka flip it; watch the locals.) Walkers pass on the other side.
  • Onsen rules at the ryokanWash and rinse completely at the seated showers BEFORE entering the bath, swimsuits are a no in traditional baths, hair up and towels stay out of the water. If anyone has visible tattoos, say so at booking: it's exactly why La Vista's private reservable baths are our alternate.
  • No tipping, anywhere, everCovered in the money tab, worth repeating: it can genuinely confuse or embarrass staff. A smile and arigatou gozaimasu is the tip.
🗣️ Ten phrases that carry the week
  • KonnichiwaHello.
  • Arigatou gozaimasuThank you (the polite full version; use it constantly).
  • SumimasenExcuse me / sorry / getting a waiter's attention. The most useful word in Japan.
  • Eigo o hanasemasu ka?Do you speak English?
  • Kore o kudasaiThis one, please (point at anything).
  • Ikura desu ka?How much is it?
  • Toire wa doko desu ka?Where is the bathroom?
  • Oishii!Delicious! (Say it at the sumo hotpot and watch faces light up.)
  • DaijoubuIt's okay / I'm fine / no thanks.
  • Kanpai!Cheers. Thanksgiving dinner requires it.

🚄 Getting between the three hotels (the transfer days)

Nothing here needs a separate booking. The two hotel-to-hotel moves ARE the two bullet-train rides already in the plan, and forwarded luggage carries two of the three. Door to door:

  • Tokyo apartment → Fuji ryokan (Wed Nov 25)Daypacks only. Morning of, hand the big bags to the MIMARU Ueno front desk to forward straight to Kyoto (they skip Fuji entirely). Short subway hop to Shinjuku, board the reserved Fuji Excursion train (about 2 hours), and the ryokan's own free shuttle meets you at Kawaguchiko Station. No heavy lifting anywhere.
  • Fuji ryokan → Kyoto apartment (Thu Nov 26)Ryokan shuttle back to the station, bullet train to Kyoto, and MIMARU Kyoto Station is directly across from the platforms: a two-minute roll to the front desk where the forwarded bags are already waiting. Best-placed hotel of the trip, on purpose.
  • Kyoto apartment → final Tokyo night (Sat Nov 28)The one day we travel with our own bags, since forwarding can't arrive same-night. Easy version: check out of the hotel across from the station, roll bags two minutes to the platform, sunset shinkansen to Tokyo, then a direct train to Ueno. It's the last night, so bags in tow barely matters.
  • Inside each city, all weekEverything is a walk or a tap-on subway ride with a Suica card in your phone (Step 6). The apartments were picked to sit inside the walkable core of each city, so hotel-to-shops is never a booking, never a taxi you have to plan.
The only ground transport you pre-book is the two airport vans in Step 3. Everything between hotels is trains you're already reserving plus the free ryokan shuttle; everything inside a city is Suica taps. Luggage forwarding is arranged at the front desk the morning you need it, about $15 a bag, no reservation.
